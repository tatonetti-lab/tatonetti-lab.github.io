Notes on conducting and writing up peer reviews

Reviewing unpublished manuscripts can be a daunting task, especially if it's your first time doing such a review. Here are some notes on conducting and then writing up a solid peer review of a manuscript. So next time a journal or your PI asks you for one, you'll have some guidelines.

Note 1: This is assuming a structure of Abstract, Introduction, Results, Discussion, Methods. Some of the very top journals (SNC) require a less rigid, more essay style that blends all these together. In that case, I’m less critical about editorializing in the results.

Note 2: This advice also doubles as tips on how to critically read any scientific publication you might be interested in.

Step 1: Read the paper and make notes.

First, I read the abstract to get an overall idea of what the paper is about and what the authors are claiming the main findings and contributions are. This is important as any major findings claimed need to be directly supported by the evidence reported in the results. I usually highlight the sentences that I think are most critical and interesting or make notes in the margins as I read through entire manuscript.

Next, I read the introduction section which is arguably the most important section of the paper. The introduction should clearly layout the purpose of the research, the prior work that this work builds upon, the related work and how this approach/method/technique is different, and then conclude with a paragraph that summarizes the major findings that are presented.

Then comes the results section. The results should clearly report on the findings of the experiments without too much methods description – just enough to understand the results being reported – and no editorializing. If the authors are providing too much methodological detail, I’ll highlight it to suggest editing later. I will also highlight any interpretation or editorializing in the discussion to suggest removal. The place for the interpretation of the results is the discussion. This is likely also where you’ll read and review the figures and the figure legends. Make sure the figures are clear and understandable, highlight or make note of anything confusing or that appears missing.

After the results, read through the discussion section. The authors should summarize their major contribution and findings in the first paragraph and then provide some interpretation. Then there may be a few paragraphs where the authors interpret their results. They should also have a discussion of limitations and opportunities for future research. This may be its own paragraph/subsection or could be discussed along with the other interpretation of the results. The most important thing about reviewing the discussion is to make note of any claims and that they are supported by the results.

Usually at this point I review everything I’ve read to make sure I get a picture of the entire paper and what my primary notes or questions were.

Finally, I review the methods. The methods should be comprehensive and clear. There should be enough detail so that an expert in the field can replicate the findings. Any code that was used should be version controlled and publicly available. All the data for the figures should also be available so that they can be easily reproduced or evaluated in a different way.

Step 2. Summarize and draft the review.

At this point we have read the paper and made a lot of notes and highlights. At this point I start drafting my review. Here is the format I use:

Summary: Write a one-paragraph summary in your own words about what the main goal, findings, and contribution of the paper is. End this paragraph with an overall assessment of the paper. E.g. “I found the manuscript to be well written and clear. However, I believe the are critical experimental data that are missing to support the authors claims.”

Major Critiques: Make a bullet point list of critiques that will require major revisions. Major revisions are changes to the research that may change the overall claims of the paper. Examples of major critiques include:

  • Missing a key piece of evidence that you think is required for them to interpret the results they way they have
  • Haven’t considered an important bias or confounder
  • Misinterpreted results or misused methods (violating assumptions without satisfactory explanation)
  • Claims unsupported by the evidence presented
  • Missing methods or methods that are insufficiently detailed
  • Etc.

Minor Critiques: Make a bullet point list of minor critiques. These are spelling errors or changes to the structure of the manuscript. Any revisions you think should be made but would not alter the interpretation of the manuscript or its major claims. Examples of minor critiques are:

  • Spelling or grammar errors
  • Structural changes to the writing of the manuscript
  • Editorialization in the results
  • Code and data availability
  • Etc.
